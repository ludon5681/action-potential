The Peripheral Nervous System

An overview of the system that allows you to interact with the world, carrying information in and responses out of your central nervous system.

The nervous system can be separated into many, many divisions. This is to be expected, as the nervous system is so detailed and important, and so it needs to have specialized systems to perform specialized functions.

The first major division is the Central Nervous System (CNS) and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS). In this section, we will be focussing on the PNS. The PNS can be divided into two systems; Sensory division and the Motor Division, which further divides into the Somatic Nervous System (SNS), and the autonomic nervous system (ANS). To explain these briefly, the motor division carries nerve impulses from the CNS to various cells (mainly muscle cells) of the body. The sensory division is in essence the opposite of the motor division. Instead of carrying impulses to peripheral regions of the body from the central nervous system, the sensory division will send impulses from peripheral organs to the CNS. The SNS controls voluntary movements of skeletal muscles. The autonomic nervous system contains the sympathetic “fight or flight” nervous system, and the parasympathetic “rest and digest” nervous system.

What does the motor division control/do?

  • Carries nerve impulses from muscle cells to CNS
  • Carries nerve impulses from CNS to muscle cells
  • Recieves nerve impulses from muscle cells
  • Recieves nerve impulses from somatic nervous system
